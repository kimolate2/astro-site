The backup landscape has shifted dramatically in recent years, with cloud storage becoming more affordable and local NAS devices more capable. Understanding the strengths and weaknesses of each approach is essential for building a reliable data protection strategy.

Cloud backup services like Backblaze, iDrive, and Carbonite offer off-site protection that guards against physical disasters like fires, floods, and theft. They run automatically in the background and maintain version history, making it easy to recover files from a specific point in time. The main drawbacks are upload speed limitations and ongoing subscription costs that can add up over years of use.

Local backup using external drives or NAS systems provides faster backup and restore speeds, complete control over your data, and no recurring fees. A properly configured NAS with RAID redundancy can survive individual drive failures without data loss. However, local backups are vulnerable to the same physical threats as your primary data if stored in the same location.

The ideal strategy combines both approaches in what's known as the 3-2-1 backup rule: three copies of your data, on two different types of media, with one copy stored off-site. This could mean keeping your primary data on your computer, a local backup on a NAS, and a cloud backup for disaster recovery. The small monthly cost of cloud storage is worthwhile insurance against worst-case scenarios.
