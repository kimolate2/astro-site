An external hard drive that isn't detected by your computer is a common issue with many possible causes, ranging from simple cable problems to serious hardware failures. Systematic troubleshooting can usually identify and resolve the issue without data loss.

Start with the basics: try a different USB cable and port. USB cables degrade over time, and a partially damaged cable may provide power but fail to establish a data connection. If you're using a USB hub, connect directly to a port on the computer instead. For drives that require external power, verify the power adapter is working and the drive's LED indicator lights up.

If the drive is powered on but not appearing in File Explorer or Finder, check Disk Management on Windows or Disk Utility on macOS. The drive may be recognized by the system but not assigned a drive letter, or it may have a file system that the current OS can't read natively. Windows can't read macOS HFS+/APFS volumes, and macOS can't write to NTFS without third-party drivers.

When the drive doesn't appear in Disk Management at all, the issue may be driver-related. Open Device Manager and look for any devices with warning icons under Disk Drives or Universal Serial Bus Controllers. Updating or reinstalling the USB drivers often resolves detection issues. If the drive clicks, beeps, or makes unusual sounds, it likely has a mechanical failure that requires professional recovery services — do not attempt to open the drive yourself.
